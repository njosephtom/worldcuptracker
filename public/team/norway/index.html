Norway World Cup 2026

UEFA · FIFA #31 · Group I

Norway return to the FIFA World Cup in 2026 for the first time in 28 years, ending one of the longest absences by a European nation from football's premier tournament. The last time Norway graced the World Cup stage was at France 1998, and the journey back has been marked by near-misses, heartbreak, and ultimately redemption through one of the most exciting generations of Norwegian footballers in the country's history. Led by the prolific Erling Haaland and the creative genius of Martin Odegaard, this Norway squad has captured the imagination of Scandinavian football fans and neutrals worldwide who are eager to see whether this golden generation can deliver on the biggest stage of all.

The qualification campaign showcased everything that makes this Norwegian side so compelling. Haaland's extraordinary goal-scoring record at club level translated emphatically to international duty, with the Manchester City striker netting consistently throughout the qualifying rounds. Odegaard, the Arsenal captain, orchestrated much of Norway's creative play from midfield, dictating tempo and unlocking defenses with his vision and passing range. But this is far more than a two-man team. The depth of Norwegian talent playing at top European clubs has created a squad with genuine quality across all positions, from the defensive solidity of Kristoffer Ajer to the midfield industry of Sander Berge and the attacking versatility of Alexander Sorloth.

Drawn into Group I alongside France, Senegal, and Iraq, Norway face a demanding set of fixtures that will test their credentials immediately. The group features the 2018 World Cup champions in France and Africa's top-ranked side in Senegal, meaning Norway will need to be at their very best from the first whistle. Yet the expanded 48-team format and the presence of Iraq as the fourth team in the group give Norway a realistic pathway to the knockout rounds, where the real adventure would begin for a nation starved of World Cup football for nearly three decades.

Tournament Outlook & Predictions

Norway's tournament outlook at the 2026 World Cup is a fascinating blend of optimism and uncertainty. On one hand, the individual quality within their squad is undeniable. These two players alone give Norway a ceiling that few teams outside the traditional powerhouses can match. On the other hand, Norway's lack of tournament experience at this level is a genuine concern, with no players in the current squad having previously appeared at a World Cup.

The tactical approach Norway are likely to adopt will be crucial to their success. In qualification, they demonstrated an ability to play pragmatically when needed while also unleashing devastating attacking football when opponents left space. Against France, Norway will almost certainly adopt a more cautious setup, looking to remain compact and hit on the counter-attack through Haaland's pace and movement. Against Senegal and Iraq, they may look to impose themselves more with possession-based football, using Odegaard as the creative fulcrum to break down organized defenses. The versatility to shift between these approaches will be key to their chances of progressing from what is a competitive group.

Prediction models typically give Norway around a 50-55% chance of advancing from the group stage, reflecting both their quality and the difficulty of their opposition. A realistic target would be to finish second in the group behind France, which would require beating Iraq and getting at least a point from one of their other two matches. If they can navigate the group stage, Norway's knockout round potential is significant. Haaland's ability to score decisive goals under pressure, combined with the team's defensive organization and tactical discipline, could make them a dangerous opponent for any side in the tournament. The romantic narrative of Norway's return to the World Cup, combined with the generational talent at their disposal, makes them one of the most intriguing teams to watch at the 2026 competition.

Head-to-Head Records

Norway's head-to-head record against their Group I opponents reveals a mixed history that provides both encouragement and warnings. Against France, Norway have an intriguing competitive record that belies the gap in overall quality between the two nations. Most famously, Norway defeated France 2-1 in a World Cup qualifier in 1998, a result that remains one of the most celebrated victories in Norwegian football history. The two sides have met numerous times in European qualification campaigns over the decades, and while France have generally dominated the overall record, Norway have shown a consistent ability to make life difficult for Les Bleus, particularly on home soil in Oslo where the hostile conditions and physical playing style have leveled the playing field on several occasions.

Against Senegal, Norway's competitive history is sparse. The two nations have met only a handful of times in international friendlies, with no particularly significant results to draw upon. This relative unfamiliarity could work in either team's favor, as it reduces the psychological baggage that can accompany more established rivalries. Norway will approach the Senegal fixture with respect for the African champions' quality but without the kind of mental block that can develop from repeated negative results. Against Iraq, Norway have virtually no competitive history, with the two nations moving in different footballing orbits for most of the modern era. The Matchday 1 fixture against Iraq represents Norway's best opportunity to secure an early victory and settle any opening-match nerves that might accompany their return to the World Cup after such a long absence.

Norway at the FIFA World Cup — History

Norway's World Cup history is modest in scale but contains moments of genuine significance that have shaped the nation's footballing identity. Their first appearance came at the 1938 World Cup in France, where they were eliminated in the first round by Italy, the eventual champions. It would be 56 years before Norway returned to the tournament, qualifying for the 1994 World Cup in the United States under Egil Olsen's distinctive long-ball tactical system. In that tournament, Norway showed resilience and organization, drawing 1-1 with Mexico and beating Ireland 0-0 on chances created despite a goalless draw, but a defeat to Italy ended their campaign in the group stage. The 1994 side, featuring players like Oyvind Leonhardsen, Henning Berg, and Tore Andre Flo, established a template of competitive determination that became synonymous with Norwegian football.

The 1998 World Cup in France represented the high-water mark of Norwegian football until now. Under Olsen's continued guidance, Norway qualified for a tournament held in the country that had been their most significant footballing rival. A famous 2-1 victory over Brazil in the group stage, with Kjetil Rekdal converting a late penalty to seal one of the greatest results in Norwegian history, demonstrated that this small Scandinavian nation could compete with the very best. Norway progressed to the Round of 16, where they lost 1-0 to Italy, ending a tournament that had captured the entire nation's attention and inspired a generation of young Norwegians to pursue football. The subsequent 28-year absence from the World Cup has been difficult for Norwegian football, with numerous near-misses in qualification campaigns that only heightened the frustration and longing for a return to the global stage.

The 2026 World Cup therefore represents not just a sporting event for Norway but a moment of national catharsis and renewal. The Haaland-Odegaard generation has broken through the ceiling that kept Norway out of major tournaments for so long, and the entire country will be watching with a mixture of pride and expectation. The hope is that this squad can match or surpass the achievements of 1998, using the Round of 16 appearance as a minimum benchmark and the quarter-finals or beyond as the aspirational target. Norwegian football has been building toward this moment for years, investing in coaching education, youth development pathways, and the cultivation of technical skills that have produced a generation of players capable of competing at the highest levels of European club football. The 2026 World Cup is the stage where all of that investment and development will be tested against the best teams on earth.

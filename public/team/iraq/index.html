Iraq World Cup 2026

AFC · FIFA #57 · Group I

Iraq's qualification for the FIFA World Cup 2026 is one of the most remarkable and emotionally resonant stories in the history of international football. The Lions of Mesopotamia return to the World Cup for the first time in 40 years, having last appeared at the 1986 tournament in Mexico. In the decades since, Iraqi football has endured wars, international sanctions, political upheaval, and infrastructure devastation that would have destroyed the footballing ambitions of most nations entirely. Yet the passion for the beautiful game in Iraq has never wavered, and this historic qualification represents a triumph of resilience, determination, and an unbreakable love for football that has sustained the nation through its darkest hours.

The qualification journey through the AFC region was a testament to the fighting spirit that defines Iraqi football. In a confederation that has grown enormously in quality and competitiveness, with established powers like Japan, South Korea, Australia, and Saudi Arabia dominating the landscape, Iraq's path to the World Cup required them to overcome opponents who possessed greater resources, more established club infrastructures, and deeper player pools. Yet the Lions of Mesopotamia drew upon their rich footballing tradition and the collective will of a nation desperate to see their flag on the World Cup stage once more, producing performances of remarkable courage and tactical intelligence throughout the qualifying campaign.

Drawn into Group I alongside France, Senegal, and Norway, Iraq face arguably the most challenging group assignment of any AFC qualifier at the tournament. France are former World Cup champions, Senegal are the reigning African champions and a perennial force, and Norway bring the firepower of Erling Haaland and the creativity of Martin Odegaard. For Iraq, simply being present at the World Cup is already a monumental achievement, but the squad and coaching staff will arrive with ambitions that extend beyond mere participation. Every match will be treated as a cup final, every moment on the pitch an opportunity to demonstrate to the world that Iraqi football has returned to the global stage with pride and purpose.

Iraq at the FIFA World Cup — History

Iraq's World Cup history is defined by a single previous appearance that has taken on almost mythological status within the country's footballing culture. The 1986 FIFA World Cup in Mexico marked Iraq's first and, until 2026, only appearance on the sport's grandest stage. In that tournament, Iraq were drawn into a group alongside Belgium, Mexico (the host nation), and Paraguay. Despite the enormous challenge of competing against established footballing nations on their first World Cup adventure, the Iraqi team acquitted themselves with honor and dignity. They lost 1-0 to Paraguay in their opening match and 2-1 to Belgium in a spirited performance, before going down 1-0 to hosts Mexico in their final group game. Though they departed without a point or a victory, the 1986 squad returned home as heroes who had represented their nation on the world stage with pride and competitive spirit.

The four decades between Iraq's first and second World Cup appearances encompass one of the most extraordinary and heartbreaking periods in the history of any footballing nation. The Gulf War of 1991, international sanctions throughout the 1990s, the 2003 invasion and subsequent conflict, and years of sectarian violence created conditions that made the development and maintenance of a competitive national football program nearly impossible. Players trained under threat of violence, infrastructure was destroyed and rebuilt multiple times, and the Iraqi football community endured personal tragedies that would have broken the spirit of a less passionate footballing culture. Yet through all of this, football remained a unifying force in Iraqi society, transcending ethnic, religious, and political divisions to bring moments of collective joy and pride.

The greatest triumph of Iraqi football during this difficult period came at the 2007 Asian Cup, where Iraq won the continental championship in a campaign that united a war-torn nation in celebration. Playing many of their matches away from home due to security concerns, the Iraqi squad produced performances of extraordinary determination and quality to defeat Australia, South Korea, and Saudi Arabia on their way to the title. The scenes of celebration in Baghdad and across Iraq after the final victory remain among the most emotionally powerful moments in the history of international sport, demonstrating the capacity of football to provide hope and unity in the most challenging circumstances. The 2026 World Cup qualification carries echoes of that 2007 triumph, representing another moment when Iraqi football has risen above adversity to achieve something that seemed impossible. This time, the stakes and the stage are even grander, and the Lions of Mesopotamia carry the hopes and dreams of an entire nation on their shoulders as they step onto the World Cup pitch for only the second time in history.

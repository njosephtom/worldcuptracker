Belgium at World Cup 2026

FIFA Rank #9 · Group G · UEFA

Belgium arrive at the 2026 FIFA World Cup with a squad that blends the tail end of one of international football's most celebrated golden generations with a wave of hungry young talent. For much of the last decade, the Red Devils sat atop the FIFA rankings, a feat that no small European nation had ever sustained for so long. Now ranked ninth in the world, Belgium enter the tournament in the United States, Canada, and Mexico determined to prove that their best football is not behind them and that a squad still featuring Kevin De Bruyne and Romelu Lukaku can mount a serious challenge for the sport's ultimate prize.

Drawn into Group G alongside Iran, Egypt, and New Zealand, Belgium face a pool that looks manageable on paper but holds genuine tactical complexity. Iran bring a disciplined defensive structure honed under years of competitive Asian football, Egypt carry the ever-present threat of their attacking talent and passionate support, and New Zealand represent a side with nothing to lose and everything to gain from their first World Cup appearance in over a decade. For head coach Domenico Tedesco, the group stage offers an opportunity to build momentum while integrating the new generation of Belgian stars into a tournament rhythm that the nation's more experienced players know all too well.

Belgium's World Cup pedigree stretches back to fourteen previous tournament appearances, including a memorable third-place finish at the 2018 World Cup in Russia. That campaign, which saw the Red Devils defeat Brazil in the quarter-finals in one of the most tactically brilliant performances in recent World Cup history, remains the high-water mark for Belgian football. The 2022 World Cup in Qatar, however, was a sobering experience. A shock group-stage exit, combined with reports of internal disharmony, left many questioning whether Belgium's window had closed. The 2026 edition represents a chance at redemption, a final opportunity for several aging stars to leave a lasting mark on the world stage.

Tournament Outlook & Predictions

Belgium's tactical identity under Domenico Tedesco has evolved significantly since the disappointment of Qatar 2022. Tedesco, who took charge in early 2023, has favored a fluid 3-4-2-1 formation that allows the team to shift into a back four when in possession and a compact five-man defensive line when out of it. This system places enormous demands on the wing-backs, where Timothy Castagne and Jeremy Doku have become crucial to providing both width in attack and defensive cover during transitions. The flexibility of this shape gives Belgium multiple avenues of attack while addressing one of their historical weaknesses: vulnerability on the counter-attack through the wide channels.

The creative heartbeat of the team remains Kevin De Bruyne, whose ability to dictate tempo from deep positions and deliver devastating final balls makes him arguably the most important player at Belgium's disposal. At thirty-four years old, De Bruyne's fitness will be carefully managed throughout the tournament, but his influence on matches remains undiminished when he is on the pitch. Behind him, the midfield axis of Youri Tielemans and Amadou Onana offers an ideal blend of technical passing quality and physical dominance. Onana, in particular, has grown into one of the Premier League's most imposing central midfielders at Aston Villa, and his ability to break up opposition attacks and drive forward with the ball makes him a vital component of Tedesco's plans.

In attack, Belgium possess an embarrassment of riches. Romelu Lukaku, the nation's all-time leading scorer, leads the line with his trademark combination of pace, power, and intelligent movement. Lukaku's record at international level speaks for itself, and his ability to bully center-backs in the penalty area gives Belgium a focal point that few other nations can match. Supporting him, Leandro Trossard and Jeremy Doku offer contrasting but equally dangerous options on the flanks, while Lois Openda provides an explosive alternative through the middle with his pressing intensity and pace in behind defensive lines.

Defensively, the inclusion of Jan Vertonghen at the age of thirty-nine speaks to both his extraordinary longevity and the relative lack of depth Belgium have in central defense. Vertonghen's reading of the game remains exceptional, but his pace has naturally declined, and Tedesco will need to ensure the team's defensive structure protects him adequately against the quicker forward lines they will face in the knockout rounds. Koen Casteels, who has been the first-choice goalkeeper since Thibaut Courtois's prolonged injury struggles, is a reliable presence who commands his area well and has proven himself capable of producing match-winning saves at the highest level.

Belgium's greatest strength heading into this tournament is their experience. The core of this squad has been through multiple major tournaments together, and that institutional knowledge of how to navigate the pressures of a World Cup cannot be underestimated. Their greatest weakness, however, is the question of whether the legs of their aging stars can carry them through what could be seven matches over the course of a month in the demanding conditions of a North American summer. Depth in certain positions, particularly at center-back and in the goalkeeping department, also remains a concern.

Head-to-Head Records

Belgium's history against their Group G opponents is relatively sparse in competitive fixtures but offers some useful context. Against Egypt, the Red Devils have met the Pharaohs on a handful of occasions, most notably in a 2018 pre-World Cup friendly in Brussels where Belgium ran out comfortable 3-0 winners. Egypt's style of play, which tends to prioritize defensive organization and rapid counter-attacks through their most dangerous attackers, will require Belgium to be patient in possession and disciplined when committing players forward. The Pharaohs are a side that can punish overconfidence, and Belgium's coaching staff will be acutely aware of the need to control tempo against them.

Against Iran, Belgium's competitive record is limited to a single World Cup encounter at the 2018 tournament in Russia, where the Red Devils secured a hard-fought 1-0 victory in their group-stage opener. That match was far from comfortable. Iran defended deep and compact, limiting Belgium to few clear-cut chances, and it took a moment of individual brilliance to break the deadlock. Iran have historically been one of the toughest teams to break down in Asian football, and their qualification for the 2026 tournament suggests that their defensive resilience has not diminished. Belgium will need to move the ball quickly and find creative solutions to unlock a side that will be content to sit behind the ball and frustrate.

New Zealand represent the least familiar opponent for Belgium, with the two nations having never met in a competitive fixture at senior level. The All Whites, as New Zealand's football team is known, have historically been a physical and well-organized side that competes fiercely for every ball. Their qualification for the 2026 World Cup is a significant achievement for Oceanian football, and they will arrive at the tournament determined to make an impact. Belgium will be overwhelming favorites in this fixture, but the Red Devils must guard against complacency, particularly if the match falls on the final matchday when fatigue and squad rotation could factor into team selection.

World Cup History

Belgium's World Cup history stretches back to the inaugural tournament in 1930, making them one of only four European nations to have participated in the very first edition of football's greatest competition. Their early World Cup appearances were largely unremarkable, with the Red Devils failing to advance beyond the group stage in their first several attempts. It was not until the 1980s that Belgian football truly announced itself on the world stage. At the 1986 World Cup in Mexico, Belgium reached the semi-finals for the first time in their history, defeating the Soviet Union and Spain along the way before falling to a Diego Maradona-inspired Argentina. That tournament remains a watershed moment in Belgian football, demonstrating that a small nation could compete with the very best when talent, organization, and team spirit aligned.

The golden generation that emerged in the 2010s transformed Belgium from perennial dark horses into genuine contenders. Led by Eden Hazard, De Bruyne, Lukaku, Courtois, and a host of world-class players plying their trade at Europe's biggest clubs, Belgium climbed to the top of the FIFA rankings and stayed there for an unprecedented period. The crowning achievement of this era was the 2018 World Cup in Russia, where Belgium finished third after defeating England in the third-place playoff. The quarter-final victory over Brazil, achieved through a masterclass in counter-attacking football orchestrated by manager Roberto Martinez, is widely regarded as one of the finest Belgian performances in the history of the sport. The Red Devils took the lead through an own goal before Romelu Lukaku's driving run set up Kevin De Bruyne for a thunderous strike that sealed the win.

The 2022 World Cup in Qatar marked a painful low point for this generation. Internal tensions within the squad were widely reported, and a disjointed team performance saw Belgium eliminated at the group stage after a shock defeat to Morocco and a goalless draw with Croatia. The fallout from that tournament led to significant changes in the coaching setup and a period of honest reflection about the direction of Belgian football. As the 2026 World Cup approaches, the Red Devils carry both the weight of past glories and the sting of recent failure, a combination that could either inspire a final glorious chapter or prove an emotional burden too heavy to bear. With fourteen World Cup appearances to their name, Belgium are among the most experienced nations in the tournament's history, and the supporters who travel to North America will be hoping that experience translates into the deep tournament run that has so far eluded this remarkable group of players.

Frequently Asked Questions

What group is Belgium in at the 2026 World Cup?

Belgium have been drawn into Group G at the 2026 FIFA World Cup, alongside Iran, Egypt, and New Zealand. The group is widely considered one of the more balanced pools in the tournament, though Belgium enter as the clear favorites based on their FIFA ranking of ninth in the world and their wealth of experience at major tournament level. The group stage will be played between June 16 and June 25, 2025, with Belgium's opening match against Egypt on June 16.

Who is Belgium's captain at the 2026 World Cup?

Kevin De Bruyne is expected to wear the captain's armband for Belgium at the 2026 World Cup. The Manchester City midfielder has been one of the most influential players in world football for over a decade and assumed the captaincy following Eden Hazard's retirement from international duty. De Bruyne's leadership on the pitch, his ability to elevate the performance of those around him, and his vast experience in high-pressure matches make him the natural choice to lead the Red Devils in what could be his final major tournament.

How many times has Belgium been to the World Cup?

The 2026 tournament will be Belgium's fifteenth appearance at a FIFA World Cup, making them one of the most frequent participants in the history of the competition. Belgium were one of the original thirteen teams to compete at the inaugural 1930 World Cup in Uruguay. Their best result remains the third-place finish at the 2018 World Cup in Russia, while they also reached the semi-finals at the 1986 tournament in Mexico. Belgium's consistent qualification record is a testament to the depth and quality of football talent that the small nation has consistently produced.

Where are Belgium's World Cup 2026 matches being played?

Belgium's group-stage matches at the 2026 World Cup will be played across venues in the United States, Canada, and Mexico. Specific venue assignments for Belgium's matches against Egypt on June 16 and Iran on June 20 have been confirmed, while the venue for their final group match against New Zealand on June 25 is still to be determined by FIFA. The 2026 World Cup is the first to be hosted across three countries and the first to feature an expanded 48-team format, meaning there will be more venues and more matches than at any previous edition of the tournament.

Who are Belgium's key players to watch at World Cup 2026?

Belgium's squad features several players who will be crucial to their chances of a deep tournament run. Kevin De Bruyne remains the creative fulcrum of the team, capable of producing moments of magic that can decide matches at the highest level. Romelu Lukaku, Belgium's all-time leading goalscorer, provides a lethal presence in the penalty area. Jeremy Doku brings electrifying pace and dribbling ability on the wing, while Amadou Onana anchors the midfield with his physical presence and ball-winning ability. Lois Openda has emerged as an exciting attacking option whose pressing and pace stretch opposition defenses. Together, this group represents a blend of proven international pedigree and emerging talent that makes Belgium a dangerous proposition for any opponent at the tournament.

Can Belgium win the 2026 World Cup?

Belgium are considered among the contenders for the 2026 World Cup, though they are not among the top favorites. The bookmakers and pundits generally place France, England, Brazil, Argentina, and Germany ahead of the Red Devils in pre-tournament predictions. However, Belgium's squad contains enough individual quality and collective tournament experience to challenge any team on their day. The key questions surround the fitness and form of their aging core players, the team's ability to maintain defensive solidity against elite opposition, and whether the painful memory of the 2022 group-stage exit serves as motivation rather than a psychological burden. If De Bruyne, Lukaku, and the supporting cast can find their best form simultaneously, Belgium are capable of reaching the latter stages and potentially going all the way.

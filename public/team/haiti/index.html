Haiti World Cup 2026

FIFA World Ranking #83 · Group C · CONCACAF

Haiti returns to the FIFA World Cup for the first time since 1974, ending a 52-year absence that stands as one of the most remarkable comeback stories in the history of the tournament. Les Grenadiers, as the national team is affectionately known, have overcome immense challenges both on and off the pitch to earn their place at the 2026 World Cup in North America. For a nation of approximately 11 million people that has endured devastating natural disasters, political upheaval, and economic hardship, qualifying for the World Cup represents far more than a sporting achievement — it is a source of immense national pride and unity that has galvanized the entire Haitian diaspora around the world.

Placed in Group C alongside Brazil (#5), Morocco (#7), and Scotland (#42), Haiti face the daunting challenge of competing against teams ranked significantly higher in the FIFA rankings. The group is undeniably one of the toughest in the tournament for Haiti, with two top-ten sides and a European qualifier standing between them and any chance of advancing. However, Haiti's CONCACAF qualifying campaign proved that this team is more than capable of rising to the occasion when the stakes are highest, and the underdog role is one that Les Grenadiers embrace with passion and determination.

The Haitian football community extends far beyond the island nation itself. A massive diaspora population, particularly concentrated in the United States, Canada, and France, ensures that Haiti will have passionate and vocal support at every match in the 2026 World Cup. The proximity of the tournament to these diaspora communities makes this a uniquely special occasion — Haitian fans in Miami, New York, Montreal, and beyond will travel in significant numbers to support their team, creating an atmosphere that promises to be one of the most vibrant and emotional of the entire competition.

Tournament Outlook & Predictions

Haiti's tournament outlook is built on resilience, collective spirit, and the freedom that comes with having nothing to lose. As the lowest-ranked team in Group C, Les Grenadiers enter every match as underdogs, but this can be a liberating position in tournament football. Without the weight of expectation that burdens higher-ranked nations, Haiti can play with freedom, expressiveness, and the kind of fearless intensity that has produced some of the greatest World Cup upsets in history. The expanded 48-team format also means that even a strong third-place finish in the group could theoretically see Haiti advance.

Tactically, Haiti play a direct, energetic style that leverages their physical athleticism and pace on the counter-attack. The team's forwards — including Duckens Nazon, Frantzdy Pierrot, and Derrick Etienne Jr — possess the speed and movement to trouble any defence on the break, particularly if opponents commit numbers forward. In midfield, Steeven Saba provides the industry and discipline needed to maintain defensive shape, while the defensive line, anchored by Carlens Arcus and Mechack Jerome, has shown the organization required to frustrate technically superior opponents for extended periods.

The key tactical challenge for Haiti will be managing the balance between defensive security and attacking threat. Against Brazil and Morocco, Haiti will likely need to defend for long stretches and remain patient for counter-attacking opportunities. Set pieces could be a significant weapon — Haiti's physical presence in the air and the unpredictability of dead-ball situations can level the playing field against more technically gifted opponents. The Matchday 1 encounter against Scotland is arguably Haiti's best chance of earning points, as the ranking gap between the two sides is the smallest of any Group C fixture involving Haiti.

Perhaps Haiti's greatest strength is the emotional energy and collective belief that flows through the squad. This is a team that has earned its place at the World Cup through sheer determination, and every player in the squad understands the significance of what they represent. The pride of an entire nation rests on their shoulders, and that responsibility has historically brought out the very best in Haitian footballers. When the crowd is behind them and the adrenaline is pumping, Haiti are capable of performances that defy their FIFA ranking and produce moments that will be remembered for generations.

Head-to-Head Records — Group C Opponents

Haiti vs Scotland

Haiti and Scotland have no recorded senior-level meetings prior to the 2026 World Cup, making their Matchday 1 encounter a completely fresh fixture for both nations. This is arguably the most important match of the group stage for both teams, as it represents each side's most realistic chance of earning three points. Haiti will look to leverage their physicality and pace against Scotland's organized but sometimes vulnerable defence, while the Scottish side will attempt to control possession and dictate the tempo. The outcome of this opening match could define the trajectory of both teams' tournament campaigns. For Haiti, a positive result would be a statement to the world that they deserve their place on this stage.

Haiti vs Brazil

Haiti and Brazil share a profound connection that extends beyond football. Brazil's large Afro-descendant population and cultural ties to the Caribbean create a unique dynamic between the two nations. On the football pitch, however, the matchup is overwhelmingly in Brazil's favour. Haiti have faced Brazil on limited occasions, and the talent gap is significant. The Matchday 2 encounter against the five-time world champions will be the ultimate test for Les Grenadiers, but it also presents the opportunity of a lifetime — the chance to compete against some of the greatest footballers on the planet on the World Cup stage. Every Haitian player will remember this match for the rest of their lives, regardless of the result.

Haiti vs Morocco

Haiti and Morocco have had minimal senior-level meetings, and their Matchday 3 encounter will be their first clash at a World Cup. Morocco, ranked #7 in the world, will be strong favourites, but Haiti can draw inspiration from the fact that Morocco themselves were once the underdog in these situations before their remarkable rise to the semi-finals of the 2022 World Cup. If the group standings create a scenario where Morocco have already qualified, Haiti could face a rotated squad — a situation that would give Les Grenadiers a greater chance of producing a competitive performance or even springing a surprise result. The Caribbean style of play, emphasizing individual skill and flair, could produce entertaining football against Morocco's organized defence.

Haiti at the FIFA World Cup — History

Haiti have appeared at one previous FIFA World Cup — the 1974 tournament in West Germany. That qualification remains one of the greatest achievements in Caribbean football history and made Haiti the first Caribbean nation to qualify for the World Cup since the tournament's inception. The 1974 squad, led by the legendary Emmanuel Sanon, captured the imagination of the Caribbean and the wider Haitian diaspora. Sanon scored against Italy in the group stage, ending Italian goalkeeper Dino Zoff's world-record run of 1,142 minutes without conceding a goal — a moment that remains one of the most celebrated in Haitian sporting history.

Despite losing all three group-stage matches (0-3 to Italy, 0-7 to Poland, and 0-4 to Argentina), Haiti's 1974 World Cup appearance left an indelible mark on the nation's identity. Emmanuel Sanon's goal against Italy became a symbol of Haitian resilience and talent, proving that even the smallest nations could produce moments of brilliance on the world's biggest stage. The 1974 squad returned home as heroes, and their legacy continues to inspire every generation of Haitian footballers that has followed.

In the 52 years since that appearance, Haiti have remained a significant force in Caribbean football despite the enormous challenges facing the nation. Les Grenadiers have been regular participants in the CONCACAF Gold Cup, reaching the quarter-finals on multiple occasions and producing memorable performances against more fancied opponents. The development of Haitian football has been aided by the diaspora, with players of Haitian descent competing in top leagues across North America and Europe, bringing their experience and skills back to the national team. The 2026 World Cup qualification represents the culmination of decades of effort and belief, and Haiti arrive in North America determined to honour the legacy of Emmanuel Sanon and the class of 1974.
